Understanding Probate in Nigeria

Probate law in Nigeria governs the legal process of administering a deceased person's estate, whether they left a will (testate) or died without one (intestate). The probate process ensures proper distribution of assets to beneficiaries while resolving any disputes that may arise.

The Probate Process in Nigeria

Steps in Estate Administration

1

Initial Application

File petition for probate (with will) or letters of administration (without will) at the probate registry of the High Court.

Documents needed: Death certificate, affidavit, list of assets, will (if any), list of beneficiaries.

2

Publication of Notice

Notice of application must be published in a newspaper for 14 days to allow for objections from interested parties.

3

Court Hearing

If no objections, the court reviews the application and may require additional documentation or clarification.

For contested cases, full hearings are conducted to resolve disputes before proceeding.

4

Grant Issuance

Court issues grant of probate (with will) or letters of administration (without will), empowering the executor/administrator to act.

5

Estate Administration

Executor/administrator collects assets, pays debts and taxes, then distributes remaining estate to beneficiaries.

Timeframe: Typically 6-12 months for simple estates, longer for complex cases or disputes.

6

Final Accounting

Executor/administrator files final account with the court showing proper distribution of all assets.

Testate Succession (With Will)

  • Will must comply with Wills Act requirements (written, signed, witnessed)
  • Executor named in will applies for grant of probate
  • Assets distributed according to testator's wishes in will
  • Certain family members may have rights regardless of will terms

Intestate Succession (Without Will)

  • No valid will exists to guide distribution
  • Administration of Estates Law or Customary Law applies
  • Court appoints administrator (usually next of kin)
  • Statutory order of beneficiaries applies (spouse, children, parents, etc.)

Notable Probate Cases in Nigeria

Adeleke v. Adeleke

SC/200/2017 (Supreme Court)

Landmark ruling on testamentary capacity, establishing that medical evidence of dementia doesn't automatically invalidate a will if executed during lucid intervals.

Will Validity Testamentary Capacity Medical Evidence

Okafor v. Nwankwo

CA/L/345/2019 (Court of Appeal)

Clarified that customary law wives must prove marriage under relevant customary law to be considered spouses for inheritance purposes under the Administration of Estates Law.

Customary Marriage Intestate Succession Spousal Rights

UBA Trustees v. Famakinwa

SC/89/2015 (Supreme Court)

Established that trustees must exercise reasonable care in managing trust assets and can be held personally liable for negligent administration of trust properties.

Trust Law Trustee Liability Estate Management

Bello v. Bello

FHC/L/CS/502/2020 (Federal High Court)

Ruled that foreign probate orders can be recognized in Nigeria through resealing process, but must comply with Nigerian probate requirements for assets located in Nigeria.

International Probate Resealing Cross-Border Estates
