The Divorce Process in Nigeria

1. Filing the Petition

The petitioner files a petition for divorce at the High Court, stating the grounds for dissolution under the Matrimonial Causes Act. Common grounds include adultery, desertion, unreasonable behavior, and living apart for at least 2 years (with consent) or 3 years (without consent).

2. Service of Process

The petition and accompanying documents are served on the respondent spouse. If the respondent cannot be located, the court may allow substituted service through publication in a newspaper.

3. Response to Petition

The respondent has 14 days (or longer if granted by the court) to file an answer to the petition. They may contest the divorce or agree but dispute ancillary matters like property division or child custody.

4. Reconciliation Attempts

Nigerian courts are required to explore possibilities of reconciliation before granting divorce. The court may refer parties to a reconciliation panel or counselor, especially when children are involved.

5. Hearing of Petition

If reconciliation fails, the court will hear evidence from both parties. In uncontested cases, the process is typically faster with minimal court appearances.

6. Decree Nisi

If satisfied with the evidence, the court grants a decree nisi (provisional divorce order). This is not yet the final dissolution of marriage.

7. Ancillary Matters

The court addresses custody, maintenance, and property division if not already settled. These matters can be resolved through negotiation, mediation, or court determination.

8. Decree Absolute

After 3 months from the decree nisi (unless shortened by the court), the petitioner can apply for the decree absolute, which finalizes the divorce and dissolves the marriage.

Important Considerations in Nigerian Divorces

Jurisdictional Requirements

  • At least one spouse must be Nigerian or domiciled in Nigeria
  • The marriage must have been celebrated under the Marriage Act or recognized under Nigerian law

Child Custody Factors

  • Welfare of the child is paramount consideration
  • Courts consider age, gender, and child's wishes (if mature enough)

Property Division

  • Courts aim for equitable (not necessarily equal) distribution
  • Contributions (financial and non-financial) are considered

Customary/Islamic Marriages

  • Different dissolution procedures apply
  • May require intervention of family elders or religious leaders

Notable Matrimonial Cases in Nigeria

Adeleke v. Adeleke

SUIT NO: LD/1349/2018 (Lagos High Court)

A landmark case on division of marital assets where the court established principles for valuing and distributing business interests acquired during marriage, setting precedent for future cases involving entrepreneur spouses.

Property Division Business Valuation

Okafor v. Okafor

SUIT NO: FCT/HC/M/1202/2019 (Abuja High Court)

A pivotal custody case that redefined the "welfare principle" in Nigerian family law, emphasizing child's educational continuity and emotional bonds with both parents over traditional assumptions about maternal preference.

Child Custody Welfare Principle

Mohammed v. Mohammed

SUIT NO: K/227/2020 (Kano High Court)

A significant Islamic law case clarifying the rights of women in talaq divorces, particularly regarding maintenance during iddah period and custody of children under Sharia law.

Islamic Divorce Women's Rights

Williams v. Williams

SUIT NO: CA/L/1093/2017 (Court of Appeal)

An important appellate decision on spousal maintenance, establishing factors Nigerian courts should consider when determining appropriate maintenance amounts, including standard of living during marriage and earning capacity.

Spousal Maintenance Appellate Decision
